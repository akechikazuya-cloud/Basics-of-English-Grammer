The Possessive Form of Nouns

We use the possessive form of a noun to show that something belongs to someone.


Rule 1: Singular Nouns

To make a singular noun possessive, add an apostrophe and an s.

This is my bed, and that is Peter's bed.
We all like Dad's cooking.
It is my job to collect everybody's plate after the meal.
The flies are buzzing around the horse's tail.
This is Susan and Jenny's room.
This is Tom's hat, and that is Tom's father's hat.


Rule 2: Plural Nouns That Do Not End in S

If a plural noun does not end in s, add an apostrophe and an s.

The children's room is always messy.
Some people's houses are bigger than ours.
Rats' tails are longer than mice's tails.
Men's voices are deeper than women's voices.


Quick Tip

When two names are joined by and and they own something together, add 's only to the second name.

Katy and Mike's house is very big. This means the house belongs to both Katy and Mike.
Joe and Sarah's dad works at the shoe factory. This means he is the father of both Joe and Sarah.

Sometimes you will see two possessive forms together, one after the other.

This is John's brother's ball. The ball belongs to John's brother.
Paul's teacher's house has a swimming pool. This is the house that belongs to Paul's teacher.


Rule 3: Plural Nouns That End in S

If a plural noun already ends in s, just add an apostrophe after the s. Do not add another s.

The pupils' desks are arranged in rows.
The boys' bedroom is bigger than the girls' bedroom.
The strong winds destroyed all the farmers' crops.
Mice's tails are shorter than rats' tails.


Rule 4: Names That End in S

When a name ends in s, you can make it possessive in two different ways. You can add an apostrophe and an s, or you can add just an apostrophe. Both ways are correct.

This is James's house.
This is James' house.
Which is Charles's bike?
Which is Charles' bike?
